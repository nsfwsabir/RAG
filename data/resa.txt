UNIT 3 — OCEAN, HYDRO AND GEOTHERMAL ENERGY
1. Ocean Energy — Overview
Ocean energy is continuously replenished by the sun and through tidal influences of the moon and sun gravitational forces. It is one of the most abundant and consistent forms of renewable energy available on Earth.

1.1 How Ocean Energy is Generated
•	Near-surface winds induce wave action and cause wind-blown currents at about 3% of the wind speed.
•	Tides cause strong currents into and out of coastal basins and rivers due to the gravitational pull of the moon and sun.
•	Ocean surface heating absorbs about 70% of incoming sunlight, which adds thermal energy to surface water causing expansion and flow.
•	Wind energy is stronger over the ocean due to less surface drag — technically, only sea breezes are classified as pure ocean energy.

1.2 Forms of Ocean Energy
•	Kinetic energy — energy of water movement (waves, tides, currents)
•	Potential energy — energy stored due to height difference (tide rise and fall)

2. Wave Energy
Wave energy is generated from the surface motion of ocean water caused by wind passing over the sea. Waves carry kinetic and potential energy that can be converted into electricity using various extraction technologies.

2.1 Sources of Ocean Wave Energy
•	Wind blowing over open ocean generates surface waves.
•	The size and energy of waves depend on wind speed, duration, and fetch (distance wind travels over water).
•	Wave energy is concentrated near the surface and decreases with depth.

2.2 Wave Energy Extraction Technologies

A. Point Absorber (OPT, Finavera)
A Point Absorber is a floating structure with a small horizontal dimension compared to its vertical dimension. It utilizes wave action at a single point.
•	Most designs resemble a buoy floating on the ocean surface.
•	One end is fixed (anchored to the seabed or sea surface) while the other end moves vertically with the wave.
•	The reciprocating (up-and-down) action is used to pump a fluid or drive a linear generator to produce usable electricity.
•	This device takes advantage of the heaving motion (up-and-down) of ocean waves.

B. Oscillating Water Column (OWC) — Energetech/Oceanlinx
An Oscillating Water Column (OWC) often resembles an "L" shape at the water surface. It is one of the most widely deployed wave energy converters.
•	Air is trapped in a chamber between the water surface and a bi-directional air turbine mounted on top of the platform.
•	As waves pass underneath: water rises with the wave crest, squeezes the air, raises air pressure, and forces air through the turbine causing it to spin.
•	When water drops with the wave trough: a partial vacuum is created, drawing air from outside through the turbine — which again causes it to spin.
•	The turbine blades on either end spin in opposite directions, but the main shaft spins in ONE direction, driving the generator to produce electricity.

C. Attenuators — Pelamis Wave Power
Attenuators are wave energy converters oriented parallel to the direction of wave travel. They capitalize on multiple motions: surge, sway, and heave.
•	Usually modular in design; rely on the flexing of joints to generate power — imagine two barges linked like wings.
•	Pelamis is a semi-submerged, articulated structure composed of cylindrical sections connected with hinged joints.
•	The wave-induced motion of joints is resisted by hydraulic rams that pump high-pressure fluid to drive hydraulic motors.
•	The hydraulic motors power electrical generators to produce electricity.
•	The dominant wavelength is an important design consideration for maximum power extraction.

D. Overtopping — Wave Dragon
The Wave Dragon, developed by Danish company Wave Dragon Aps, is a concept of the overtopping type of wave energy converter.
•	Incoming waves flow up a ramp into a reservoir above sea level.
•	Water then drains back to sea level through hydro-electric turbines, generating electricity.
•	"Reflector arms" focus and channel incoming waves towards the ramp, increasing energy captured.
•	Three-step energy conversion: Overtopping (absorption) → Storage (reservoir) → Power take-off (hydro turbines).

Key Point: Wave energy extraction technologies differ in their approach — some use the vertical heaving motion, others use air compression, articulation, or overtopping. The choice depends on site conditions, wave climate, and scale of deployment.

3. Tidal Energy
Tidal energy harnesses the kinetic and potential energy from the rise and fall of ocean tides. It is one of the most predictable and reliable forms of renewable energy.

3.1 Physics of Tides
•	Tides are caused by the combined gravitational pull of the sun and moon, and the centrifugal force of the Earth-Moon system rotation.
•	When a landmass lines up with the Earth-Moon system, the water around it is at HIGH TIDE.
•	When a landmass is at 90° to the Earth-Moon system, the water around it is at LOW TIDE.
•	There are TWO high tides and TWO low tides during each full rotation of the Earth (approximately 24 hours).

Spring and Neap Tides
•	Spring Tides (HIGH): Occur when the Sun, Moon, and Earth are aligned (either on the same side or opposite sides). The gravitational pull is combined, producing larger tides.
•	Neap Tides (LOW): Occur when the Sun and Moon are at 90° to each other. Their gravitational forces partially cancel, producing smaller tides.
•	Flood Currents: Tidal currents moving IN toward the coast.
•	Ebb Currents: Tidal currents receding OUT from the coast.

3.2 Components of a Tidal Power Plant
1.	Power house — contains turbines, generators, and control equipment
2.	Dam or barrage — forms the tidal basin
3.	Sluice ways — control water flow from basin to sea and vice versa
4.	Turbines — convert water flow into mechanical rotation
5.	Electric generators — convert mechanical rotation into electricity
6.	Auxiliary components — transformers, transmission lines, monitoring systems

3.3 Tidal Barrage — Types
Tidal barrages are typically dams built across an estuary or bay. They consist of turbines, sluice gates, embankments, and ship locks. Ideal sites are at narrow channels with high variation between high and low tides.

A. Single Basin System
The simplest tidal power system using one enclosed basin.
•	Ebb Generation: During flood tide, the basin fills through open sluice gates. Gates are then closed, trapping water. When the sea tide has ebbed sufficiently (low enough external water level), the trapped water is released through turbines, generating electricity as it flows out.
•	Flood Generation: The basin is emptied before high tide. Water is allowed to flow IN through the turbines at flood tide, generating electricity as the tide rises.
•	Two-Way Generation: Turbines generate during both flood and ebb tides. Sluice gates and turbines are managed to generate power in both directions, maximizing output.

B. Double Basin System
Uses two interconnected basins. Similar in operation to ebb generation, but a proportion of the generated electricity is used to pump water into a second basin — allowing energy storage. This enables more continuous power generation.

3.4 Estimation of Energy and Power

Single Basin Tidal System — Key Formulas
Work done by water: W = (1/2) x rho x g x A x R^2
Where: rho = density of sea water (~1025 kg/m³), g = 9.81 m/s², A = basin surface area (m²), R = tidal range (m)

If turbine cut-off head r is given: W = (1/2) x rho x g x A x (R^2 - r^2)

Average theoretical power: Pav = W / t  where t = 22,350 s (6h 12.5min per tidal period)

Simplified: Pav/A = 0.225 x R^2 (watts per m^2 of basin area)

Actual power: P = turbine-generator efficiency x Pav

Double Cycle System — Key Formulas
Volume of basin: V = A x h0  (A = cross-sectional area, h0 = tidal height difference)

Average discharge: Q = A x h0 / t  (t = duration of generation per cycle)

Power at any instant: P = (eta_0 x rho x Q x h) / 75 x 0.736  kW

3.5 Solved Problems — Tidal Power

Problem 1: Single basin, A = 30×10⁶ m², R = 12 m, r = 3 m, efficiency = 76%

Solution:
W = (1/2) × 1025 × 9.8 × 30×10⁶ × (144 − 9) = 40,682,250 × 10⁶ J
Pav = W/22350 = 1820.23 × 10⁶ W
Energy = 1820.23 × 10⁶ × 3600/1000 = 6552.83 × 10⁶ kWh
Actual Power = 6552.83 × 10⁶ × 0.76 = 4980 × 10⁶ kWh

Problem 2: Double cycle, h0 = 8.5 m, A = 0.5 km², t = 3 hrs, h = 8 m, η = 72%

Solution:
V = 0.5×10⁶ × 8.5 = 4.25×10⁶ m³
Q = 4.25×10⁶ / (3×3600) = 393.5 m³/s
P = (1025 × 393.5 × 8 × 0.72)/75 × 0.736 = 22,827 kW
Energy/cycle = 22,827 × 3 = 68,481 kWh
Yearly output = 68,481 × 705 = 48.27 × 10⁶ kWh/year

3.6 Pros and Cons of Tidal Power
Advantages	Limitations
Mature technology (~50 years of operation)	High construction costs for barrages
Highly reliable and predictable energy source	Environmental impacts on marine life and estuaries
Tidal current turbines utilize both ebb and flood tides	Low power output vs. coal or nuclear plants
Current turbines are not large dam structures	Tidal turbine technology is still in development
Zero fuel costs during operation	Installation and maintenance challenges offshore
Long operational lifespan (50+ years for barrages)	Environmental impacts of turbines still being studied

4. Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC)
OTEC exploits the temperature difference between warm surface water and cold deep ocean water to drive a heat engine and generate electricity.

4.1 Introduction and Principle
•	Ocean water stores much more heat than the atmosphere.
•	Thermo-cline circulation creates temperature differences throughout Earth's oceans.
•	Surface water temperature: ~20–25°C; Deep water temperature: ~5–10°C.
•	This small temperature gradient is sufficient to operate a low-temperature power cycle.
•	Requires a temperature differential of at least 20°C between surface and deep water — available in tropical oceans.

4.2 Closed-Cycle OTEC
Uses a working fluid with a low boiling point (e.g., Ammonia, NH₃ with boiling point of −33°C).
•	Step 1: Warm surface water heats the working fluid in an evaporator, turning it into vapour.
•	Step 2: The vapour expands through a turbine, generating mechanical energy which drives a generator to produce electricity.
•	Step 3: The spent vapour is condensed back to liquid in a condenser cooled by cold deep-ocean water.
•	Step 4: The liquid working fluid is pumped back to the evaporator and the cycle repeats.
•	The working fluid never comes in direct contact with seawater — it is a closed loop.

4.3 Open-Cycle OTEC
•	Uses warm surface seawater DIRECTLY as both the heat source and working fluid.
•	Step 1: Warm seawater is pumped into a low-pressure vacuum chamber, causing it to flash-boil (evaporate) at low temperatures.
•	Step 2: The steam produced drives a turbine connected to a generator.
•	Step 3: The steam is condensed by exposure to cold deep-ocean water.
•	Bonus: If cold deep water condenses the steam without direct contact, the condensate is desalinated fresh water — a valuable byproduct!

4.4 Advantages and Disadvantages of OTEC
Advantages	Disadvantages
Extremely benign environmental impact	Very low thermal efficiency due to small temperature gradient
No dependency on oil or fossil fuels	Only suitable in equatorial/tropical waters
Minimal maintenance costs vs. conventional plants	Only moderate power outputs are available
Open cycle can produce desalinated fresh water	Not economically competitive with conventional plants yet
Continuous availability (24/7 operation)	Extremely long cold water pipes are costly to manufacture/install

5. Geothermal Energy
Geothermal energy is heat energy stored within the Earth's crust. It originates from the Earth's formation and from the decay of radioactive materials within the Earth.

5.1 Sources of Earth's Internal Energy
•	70% comes from the decay of radioactive nuclei with long half-lives embedded within the Earth (e.g., Uranium-238, Thorium-232).
•	Some energy is residual heat left over from Earth's formation (gravitational compression heat).
•	A small fraction comes from meteorite impacts.

5.2 Types of Geothermal Resources
7.	Hot Water Reservoirs: Underground reservoirs of hot water. Common in the US but more suited for space heating than electricity production.
8.	Natural Steam Reservoirs: Holes dug into the ground release dry steam to the surface. Rare but ideal for electricity generation.
9.	Geopressurized Reservoirs: Brine saturated with natural gas, stored under pressure from overlying rock. Used for both heat and natural gas.
10.	Normal Geothermal Gradient: At any location on Earth, temperature increases by +30°C per km of depth. At 20,000 ft, temperature is ~190°C above surface — enough for electricity, but no economical technology yet.
11.	Hot Dry Rock (HDR): Gradient of +40°C/km. Water is injected, heated by hot rocks, and returned to surface for power generation.
12.	Molten Magma: No technology currently exists to tap the heat stored in magma.

5.3 Direct Uses of Geothermal Energy (Below 150°C)
When geothermal temperature is below 150°C, the energy is used directly for:
•	Space heating of buildings
•	Air conditioning systems
•	Industrial processes (food processing, chemical manufacturing)
•	Drying of agricultural products
•	Greenhouses for year-round crop growing
•	Aquaculture (fish farming at controlled temperatures)
•	Hot water supply for residential and commercial use
•	Resorts, spas, and swimming pools
•	Melting snow on roads and runways

How Direct Use Works
•	Water is sent down an injection well and heated by Earth's natural warmth.
•	A heat pump extracts the heat from underground water and transfers it to heat the building.
•	After the heat is extracted, the cooled water is injected back into the Earth to maintain pressure and prevent land subsidence.

5.4 Types of Geothermal Power Plants

A. Dry Steam (Vapour Dominated) Plants
•	The simplest and oldest type of geothermal power plant.
•	Steam from underground reservoirs is piped directly to the surface to drive turbines.
•	The steam must be filtered to remove rock particles before entering the turbine.
•	After driving the turbine, the steam is condensed and returned underground.
•	Example: The Geysers in California — the largest geothermal installation in the world.

B. Flash Steam (Single Flash and Double Flash) Plants
•	These systems pump deep, high-pressured hot water (>180°C) to the surface.
•	High-pressure water is transported to low-pressure flash chambers where it rapidly "flashes" (boils) into steam.
•	The resulting steam drives the turbines to generate electricity.
•	The remaining water and steam are injected back into the geothermal reservoir.
•	Double Flash: Two stages of flashing extract more energy from the same water, increasing efficiency by 15–25%.

C. Binary Cycle Plants
•	Used where water temperatures are moderate (125°F–360°F / 50°C–180°C) — too low for flash systems.
•	The geothermal water passes through a heat exchanger alongside an organic fluid (like butane, pentane, or isopentane) with a much lower boiling point.
•	The heat causes the organic fluid to vaporize and drive the turbine.
•	The geothermal water never contacts the turbine — it is completely separated.
•	This process produces NO emissions — the water is fully re-injected underground.
•	This is the fastest-growing geothermal technology due to its applicability at lower temperatures.

D. Hot Dry Rock (HDR) Plants
•	Used in areas where there is no underground water but rocks are very hot.
•	Cold pressurized water is injected down one well into hot dry rocks (>200°F).
•	The hot rocks heat the water, which is then pumped back to the surface via production wells.
•	The hot water or steam is then used in a binary cycle to generate electricity.
•	After use, the cooled water is re-injected to be heated again — a closed loop.

5.5 Geothermal Heat Pumps
•	Produces 4 times the energy it consumes (COP = 4).
•	Initially higher installation cost but maintenance cost is 1/3 of conventional heating systems.
•	Reduces electricity bills significantly over the lifetime.
•	Can be installed with special low-interest loan programs.

5.6 Harmful Effects of Geothermal Energy
•	Brine can salinize soil if geothermal water is not re-injected after heat extraction.
•	Extracting large amounts of water can cause land subsidence (ground sinking).
•	Land subsidence can increase local seismic activity.
•	Plants that don't re-inject water can release H₂S (hydrogen sulfide — "rotten egg" gas), which is toxic in large quantities.
•	Noise pollution during drilling operations.
•	Risk of blowouts — one well "blew its top" 10 years after construction, throwing hundreds of tons of rock, mud, and steam into the atmosphere.

5.7 Positive Attributes of Geothermal Energy
•	Useful minerals like zinc and silica can be extracted from underground water as a byproduct.
•	Geothermal energy is "homegrown" — reduces dependence on imported fuel.
•	Creates local jobs in drilling, plant operation, and maintenance.
•	Cost: 4–8 cents per kWh in large plants — nearly competitive with conventional sources.
•	Geothermal plants can operate 90–100% of the time (vs. coal 75%, nuclear 65%).
•	Flash and Dry Steam plants emit 1,000–2,000x LESS CO₂ than fossil fuel plants.
•	CO₂ emissions: Geothermal = 13.38 g/kWh vs. Natural Gas = 453 g/kWh, Oil = 906 g/kWh, Coal = 1,042 g/kWh.
•	Binary and HDR plants have NO gaseous emissions at all.
•	Low land requirement: 400 m² can produce a gigawatt of energy over 30 years.

5.8 Economics of Geothermal Energy
Cost of geothermal energy depends on:
•	Temperature and depth of the resource
•	Type of resource (steam, hot water, HDR)
•	Chemistry of the geothermal fluid (corrosive fluids increase costs)
•	Permeability of the reservoir rock
•	Size of the plant (larger plants = lower cost per kWh)
•	Climatic conditions at the site
•	Topography of the site (affects drilling and piping costs)
•	Proximity of transmission lines (affects grid connection costs)

CO₂ Comparison Summary: Geothermal: 13.38 g/kWh | Natural Gas: 453 g/kWh | Oil: 906 g/kWh | Coal: 1042 g/kWh
Geothermal energy is one of the cleanest available sources of base-load power.



UNIT 4 — BIOMASS ENERGY
1. Biomass — Introduction
Biomass is an organic material stored in the form of organic compounds made of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and other elements in plants and animals. It is one of the oldest and most widely used sources of renewable energy.

1.1 Sources of Biomass
Category	Examples
Wood	Trees, bushes, wood residue, sawdust, wood chips
Wastes	Municipal solid waste (MSW), process waste, sewage, industrial residues
Crops	Starch crops (corn, wheat), sugar crops (sugarcane, beet), energy crops
Aquatic Plants	Algae, water hyacinth, seaweed
Agricultural residues	Bagasse (sugarcane residue), rice husk, straw, corn stalks
Animal residues	Cattle dung, poultry waste, slaughterhouse waste

Bagasse
Bagasse is the fibrous residue remaining after sugarcane stalks are crushed to extract sugar juice. It is one of the most important agricultural biomass fuels, especially in sugar-producing countries. It is widely used in co-generation plants at sugar mills.

Wood Chips and Sawdust
Wood chips and sawdust are by-products of forestry and wood processing industries. They are easy to handle, store, and transport. They can be directly combusted or converted into pellets and briquettes for higher energy density.

Municipal Solid Waste (MSW)
MSW includes everyday waste from households, commercial establishments, and industries. The organic fraction of MSW contains significant energy potential. Incineration, gasification, and anaerobic digestion are used to recover energy from MSW.

2. Biofuels
Biofuels are fuels derived from the carbon fixation of biomass. They are considered renewable because the carbon they release was recently absorbed from the atmosphere by plants.

2.1 Types of Biofuels

A. Biodiesel
•	Produced from vegetable oils (soybean, palm, canola, jatropha) using the process of TRANSESTERIFICATION.
•	Transesterification reacts vegetable oils or animal fats with an alcohol (usually methanol) in the presence of a catalyst to produce fatty acid methyl esters (FAME) — biodiesel.
•	Can be blended with conventional diesel (B5, B20, B100) for use in existing diesel engines.

B. Green Diesel
•	Produced from the fractional distillation of oils from canola, algae, jatropha, etc.
•	Unlike biodiesel, green diesel undergoes hydrocracking/hydrotreatment to produce a product chemically identical to petroleum diesel.
•	Has higher energy density and better cold-weather properties than biodiesel.

C. Bio-Alcohols (Bioethanol)
•	Produced by the action of microorganisms and enzymes through the FERMENTATION of sugars, starches, and cellulose.
•	Common feedstocks: sugarcane (Brazil), corn (USA), wheat (Europe), cassava, switchgrass.
•	Bioethanol can be blended with gasoline (E10, E85) or used in flexible-fuel vehicles.

D. Biogas
•	Produced by the ANAEROBIC DIGESTION of biodegradable waste materials (organic matter broken down in the absence of oxygen).
•	Primary component: Methane (CH₄) — about 55–65% of biogas composition.
•	The solid byproduct (digestate) can be used as a biofuel or a highly effective organic fertilizer.

E. Solid Biofuels
•	Biomass is burnt directly in a furnace to generate heat.
•	Loose biomass (sawdust, rice husk) is densified by PELLETIZATION or BRIQUETTING to increase energy density and ease of handling.
•	Examples: wood pellets, charcoal, torrefied biomass.

3. Advantages and Limitations of Biomass
Advantages	Limitations
Abundant and widely distributed source of energy	Dispersed and land-intensive — requires large land areas
Easy to convert to high-energy portable fuels (gas/liquid)	Low energy density compared to fossil fuels
Economical — often uses waste materials as feedstock	Collection, storage, and transport costs can be high
Biomass cultivation restores and improves waste lands	Direct combustion causes air pollution and contributes to global warming
Commercial use reduces waste disposal problems	Water requirement for growing energy crops can be significant
Provides jobs in rural areas (agriculture, collection, processing)	Competition with food crops for agricultural land
Fermentation/pyrolysis causes less environmental impact than combustion	Seasonal availability of some biomass types
Nitrogen-rich bio-digested residue improves soil fertility	High capital cost for advanced conversion technologies

4. Biomass Energy Conversion Processes
Biomass can be converted into useful forms such as heat, gaseous/liquid/solid fuels, and organic chemicals. The conversion process selected depends on temperature, pressure, available micro-organisms, and desired end product.

4.1 Thermochemical vs. Biochemical Processes
Parameter	Thermochemical Process	Biochemical Process
Biomass state	Used in dry condition	Used in wet condition
Temperature	Higher temperatures required	Ambient/low temperature
End products	Solid, liquid, or gaseous fuel (depends on T and P)	Primarily biogas (methane)
Residues	Char (carbon-rich solid)	Good biological fertilizer
Main processes	Combustion, gasification, pyrolysis	Anaerobic digestion, fermentation

5. Direct Combustion
Direct combustion of biomass is the MOST COMMON method of energy generation from biomass worldwide. It applies to solid biomass including cultivated and waste biomass.

5.1 Process
•	Biomass is burned in a furnace/boiler at high temperatures (800–1000°C).
•	The heat released is used for cooking, industrial heating, steam generation, and electricity production.
•	In a steam turbine system: Biomass → Storage → Preparation/Processing → Furnace/Boiler → Steam → Turbine → Generator → Electricity

5.2 Incineration
•	Incineration is the process of burning waste materials completely to ashes.
•	The heat generated is either used to generate electricity or provide industrial/space heating.
•	Controlled incineration with pollution control systems is used in Waste-to-Energy (WTE) plants.
•	Drawback: Burning biomass leads to air pollution and contributes to global warming via CO₂ release.

6. Thermochemical Conversion
In thermochemical conversion, biomass is converted into chemical energy forms using heat as the dominant driving force. The process includes gasification and pyrolysis. End products depend on oxygen availability and conversion temperature.

6.1 Gasification
Gasification converts solid biomass into a combustible gas (producer gas/syngas) using heat, pressure, and steam with controlled/limited oxygen.

Products of Gasification:
•	Hydrocarbon gases — Synthesis gas or Syngas (CO, H₂, CH₄)
•	Hydrocarbon liquids — bio-oils and tars
•	Char — carbon black and ash (solid residue)

Syngas Composition: Primarily carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H₂) — more than 85% by volume, with smaller quantities of CO₂ and CH₄. Syngas can be used directly in gas turbines, IC engines, or further processed to produce methanol, hydrogen, or synthetic diesel.

Gasification Depends on:
•	Gasification chamber atmosphere (level of oxygen/air content)
•	Reactor design — updraft, downdraft, cross draft, or fluidized bed
•	Internal and external heating — operating temperature

Coal Gasification Reactions:
C + O2 → CO2  (exothermic - rapid combustion)
C + 1/2 O2 → CO  (exothermic - rapid partial combustion)
C + H2O → CO + H2  (endothermic - water-gas reaction)
C + CO2 → 2CO  (endothermic - Boudouard reaction)
CO + H2O → CO2 + H2  (slightly exothermic - water-gas shift reaction)
CO + 3H2 → CH4 + H2O  (exothermic - methanation)
C + 2H2 → CH4  (exothermic - direct methanation)

6.2 Types of Gasifiers

A. Updraft (Counter-Current) Gasifier
•	The SIMPLEST type of gasifier.
•	Air enters at the BOTTOM, gas exits at the TOP (counter-current to biomass flow).
•	Zones from bottom to top: Oxidation (Hearth) → Reduction → Pyrolysis → Drying
•	Near the grate: Combustion occurs.
•	Middle zone: Reduction reactions occur.
•	Upper region: Feedstock is heated and undergoes pyrolysis by radiation and convection from lower zones.
•	Tars and volatiles are carried upward in the gas stream. Ash is removed from the bottom.

Advantages (Updraft)	Limitations (Updraft)
Simple design and construction	Greater sensitivity to tar and moisture content
High charcoal burnout efficiency	Long startup time
Internal heat exchange → lower gas exit temperature	Poor reaction capability under load changes
Higher overall equipment efficiency	Channeling can cause oxygen breakthrough and explosions
Gas produced has no ash content	Suitable only for tar-free feedstocks
Small pressure drop across the gasifier	Not suitable for high volatile fuels

B. Downdraft (Co-Current) Gasifier
•	Air and biomass flow in the SAME DIRECTION (downward).
•	Zones from top to bottom: Drying → Pyrolysis (350–600°C) → Oxidation (1000–1200°C) → Reduction (700–1000°C)
•	Raw products pass through the high-temperature oxidation zone, which CRACKS most tars into gaseous hydrocarbons — producing cleaner gas.
•	Heat from the combustion zone causes wood chips to lose 70–80% of their weight.
•	Gases burn with air to form CO, CO₂, H₂, and H₂O maintaining temperatures of 1000–1200°C.
•	Product gases undergo reduction with char to produce CO, H₂, and CH₄.
•	The producer gas can be used for thermal applications. For IC engines, cleaning and cooling is required.
•	Raw gas contains varying amounts of ash, char particles, and moisture.

Advantages (Downdraft)	Limitations (Downdraft)
Flexible gas production, adapts well to load changes	Taller design required
Less sensitivity to charcoal, dust, and tar content	Not suitable for smaller particle size feedstocks
Relatively clean gas (low tar content)	Higher gas exit temperature than updraft
Suited for a variety of biomass types	Requires pre-drying of feedstock to <20% moisture

C. Cross-Draft Gasifier
•	Air enters through a WATER-COOLED NOZZLE mounted on one SIDE of the firebox.
•	The combustion and reduction zone is confined near the air nozzle.
•	Operates at very HIGH TEMPERATURES.
•	Responds rapidly to changes in gas production — highly FLEXIBLE.
•	Gas exits horizontally at 90° to the air inlet direction.

Advantages (Cross-Draft)	Limitations (Cross-Draft)
Shorter design height	Higher exit gas temperature
Quick response to varying loads	Lower CO₂ reduction, hence lower efficiency
Flexible gas production control	High sensitivity to slag formation
Suitable for high-flow applications	Low quality gas — not widely used nowadays

D. Fluidized Bed Gasifier
Fluidization promotes better contact between the gas (fluid) and solid biomass particles, leading to highly uniform and efficient gasification.
•	Higher surface area of contact between fluid and solid per unit volume.
•	Higher relative velocities between fluid and dispersed solid phase.
•	Higher levels of mixing of the particulate phase.
•	Frequent particle-particle and particle-wall collisions ensure uniform heat transfer.
•	Uniform temperature throughout the chamber: 700–950°C.

Advantages (Fluidized Bed)	Limitations (Fluidized Bed)
Air is used instead of oxygen — lower cost/power consumption	Lower calorific value of gas (1100–1250 kcal/Nm³)
Lower temperature operation, reliable and robust	Higher unburnt content in the ash
In-situ removal of sulphur and trace elements	More complex design and operation
Dry granular ash — easy removal	Requires careful particle size control of feedstock

Comparison of All Gasifier Types
Type	Advantages	Limitations
UPDRAFT	Small pressure drop, high efficiency, less slag tendency	High tar/moisture sensitivity, long startup, channeling risk
DOWNDRAFT	Flexible, less tar, good for IC engines	Taller design, not for small particles, needs dry feed
CROSSDRAFT	Short design, quick response, flexible	High slag sensitivity, high exit temperature, low efficiency
FLUIDIZED BED	Uniform temp, in-situ S removal, lower cost	Lower gas CV, higher unburnt ash, complex operation

6.3 Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis involves heating biomass (wood, agricultural waste) at temperatures of 500–900°C in the COMPLETE ABSENCE OF OXYGEN. The biomass thermally decomposes into three products:
•	Gas — combustible gases (syngas, CO, H₂, CH₄)
•	Bio-oil (liquid) — a viscous brown liquid containing complex organic compounds; can be upgraded to transportation fuels
•	Char (solid) — carbon-rich solid residue; can be used as fuel (charcoal) or soil amendment (biochar)

Key Advantage: CO₂ is NOT generated during pyrolysis (no oxygen present), making it environmentally superior to combustion.
Key Limitation: The biomass must be heated to very high temperatures, requiring significant energy input.

7. Biochemical Conversion
In biochemical processes, bacteria, enzymes, and other microorganisms are used to transform biomass into energy (methane, ethanol). These processes occur at ambient or low temperatures and are suitable for wet biomass.

7.1 Fermentation
Fermentation is the decomposition of complex organic molecules under the action of microorganisms (yeast, bacteria, enzymes).
•	Classic example: Conversion of grains and sugar crops (glucose) into ETHANOL (C₂H₅OH) and CO₂ in the presence of yeast.
C6H12O6  →  2 C2H5OH  +  2 CO2  (yeast/bacteria action)
•	Ethanol is then distilled and dehydrated for use as bioethanol fuel.
•	Used widely in Brazil (sugarcane ethanol) and USA (corn ethanol).

7.2 Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is the biological breakdown of organic matter in the ABSENCE OF OXYGEN by anaerobic microorganisms. It produces BIOGAS (primarily methane).

Four Stages of Anaerobic Digestion:
13.	HYDROLYSIS: Complex molecules (carbohydrates, fats, proteins) are broken down into simpler molecules (sugars, fatty acids, amino acids) by hydrolytic bacteria.
14.	ACIDOGENESIS: Simple molecules are further broken down into volatile fatty acids, carbonic acids, alcohols, hydrogen, and CO₂ by acidogenic bacteria.
15.	ACETOGENESIS: Volatile fatty acids and alcohols are converted into acetic acid (CH₃COOH), hydrogen (H₂), and CO₂ by acetogenic bacteria.
16.	METHANOGENESIS: Acetic acid and hydrogen/CO₂ are converted into METHANE (CH₄) and CO₂ by methanogenic archaea.

Chemical Reactions in Anaerobic Digestion:
C6H12O6  →  3CO2  +  3CH4  (glucose → biogas)
CO2  +  4H2  →  CH4  +  2H2O  (hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis)
CH3COOH  →  CH4  +  CO2  (acetoclastic methanogenesis)

Feedstocks for Biogas Production:
•	Human and animal waste (cowdung, poultry manure, pig slurry)
•	Plant residues and crop waste
•	Food waste and kitchen scraps
•	Municipal solid waste (organic fraction)
•	Industrial organic effluents
•	Sewage sludge from wastewater treatment

All feedstocks should have HIGH MOISTURE CONTENT for optimal anaerobic digestion.

8. Biogas — Features and Composition

8.1 What is Biogas?
•	Biogas is produced by DIGESTION — a biological process occurring in the ABSENCE of O₂ (anaerobic) and in the PRESENCE of anaerobic organisms at atmospheric pressure and temperatures of 35–70°C.
•	The container in which digestion occurs is called a DIGESTER.
•	When organic matter undergoes fermentation, anaerobic bacteria extract oxygen by decomposing biomass at temperatures up to 65°C with 80–95% moisture content.
•	Biogas is a CLEAN FUEL that can be obtained from animal residues, domestic and farm wastes.
•	It is an important renewable energy source especially for rural areas.

8.2 Typical Biogas Composition
Component	Percentage	Notes
Methane (CH₄)	55–65%	Primary fuel component — combustible
Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)	35–45%	Non-combustible — reduces energy content
Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)	0.5–1.0%	Corrosive and toxic — must be removed
Water Vapour (H₂O)	~1%	Condensation issue in pipes

Average Calorific Value: 20 MJ/m³ (or approximately 4,500–4,700 kcal/m³)
Typical CH₄ content: 60% in standard biogas composition

9. Biogas Plants — Types and Components

9.1 Components of a Biogas Plant
Inlet Pipe: The slurry (feedstock + water) is moved into the digester through the inlet pipe or tank.
Mixing Tank: Feed material (e.g., dung) is gathered in the mixing tank. Sufficient water is added and thoroughly mixed until a homogeneous slurry is formed.
Digester: The main underground tank where the slurry is fermented. Biogas is produced through bacterial action inside the digester.
Gas Holder/Storage Dome: The biogas produced collects in the gas holder. It stores the gas until it is transported to the utilization point.
Outlet Pipe: The spent slurry (digestate) is discharged through the outlet pipe or opening into the outlet/overflow tank.
Gas Pipeline: Carries the biogas from the gas holder to the utilization point — stove, lamp, IC engine, or generator.

9.2 Fixed Dome Type Biogas Plant (Deenbandhu/Chinese Design)
In the fixed dome design, the gas storage dome is a permanent, fixed structure built into the top of the underground digester tank.
•	The slurry (cowdung + water) is fed through the inlet chamber.
•	As gas is produced, pressure builds up in the fixed dome.
•	Gas pressure pushes slurry from the digester into the outlet displacement chamber.
•	The drop in slurry level in the digester indicates gas production.
•	Gas is accessed via the gas valve at the top of the dome.
•	The spent slurry (digestate/manure) flows out through the overflow/outlet tank.

Advantages (Fixed Dome)	Limitations (Fixed Dome)
Low capital cost	Gas released at VARIABLE pressure (difficult to control)
Low maintenance cost	Identifying defects in the gas holder is DIFFICULT (underground)
Space above the dome can be utilized	Higher temperature retention in winter (dome underground)
Longer life span	Requires MORE excavation work
No corrosion issues (masonry dome)	Cracks in dome can cause gas leakage

9.3 Floating Drum Type Biogas Plant (KVIC/Indian Design)
In the floating drum design, a cylindrical drum made of mild steel floats on the slurry surface and rises or falls with the amount of gas collected.
•	Consists of a deep masonry well (digester tank T) divided by a partition wall into two compartments.
•	The cylindrical dome H is made of mild/stainless steel and FLOATS on the slurry, collecting gas underneath.
•	Drum RISES as gas collects and FALLS as gas is consumed.
•	Constant gas pressure is maintained by the weight of the drum over unit area.
•	The inlet is HIGHER than the outlet tank, creating hydrostatic pressure to move slurry through the system.
•	Maximum gas pressure attained: 8–10 cm water column.
•	Slurry ferments for about 50 days before being discharged as digestate/manure for cultivation.

Advantages (Floating Drum)	Limitations (Floating Drum)
Slurry constantly submerged below dome — equalized pressure	Higher cost (fabricated steel dome)
No danger of excessive pressure buildup	Dome exposed to sunlight/weather — heat loss in winter
No danger of mixing biogas with external air → NO explosion risk	Flexible outlet pipe needed — requires regular maintenance
Gas released at CONSTANT and UNIFORM pressure	Shorter life span (steel corrodes)
Identifying defects in gas holder is EASY (visible drum)	Floating drum restricts use of space above
Gas does NOT leak through slurry (natural seal)	Higher maintenance cost

9.4 Fixed Dome vs. Floating Drum — Comparison
Parameter	Fixed Dome	Floating Drum
Gas Pressure	Variable (changes as gas builds/consumed)	Constant (governed by drum weight)
Defect Identification	Difficult (underground dome)	Easy (visible drum movement)
Maintenance Cost	Low	High
Capital Cost	Low	High (same capacity)
Space Utilization	Space above dome can be used	Floating drum blocks space above
Winter Performance	Better (dome underground retains heat)	Poorer (drum exposed to cold)
Life Span	Longer (masonry construction)	Shorter (steel corrodes over time)
Excavation Required	More	Less
Explosion Risk	Slight (variable pressure)	Nil (natural pressure equalization)

10. Operational Parameters of Biogas Plant
For optimal biogas production, several operational parameters must be carefully controlled:

Parameter 1: Temperature
•	Methane-forming bacteria work optimally in the temperature range of 20–55°C.
•	Raising temperature ACCELERATES gas production, but the methane content reduces.
•	Gas production DECREASES below 20°C and almost STOPS at 10°C.
•	Mesophilic range (35–40°C): Most common and stable operation.
•	Thermophilic range (50–55°C): Faster digestion but requires more heating energy.

Parameter 2: Pressure
•	Optimum operating pressure: 1.2 bar.
•	Excess pressure releases gas from the slurry and causes leaks at joints.
•	Addition of oxygen causes EXPLOSION risk — the system must remain sealed and oxygen-free.

Parameter 3: Solid to Moisture Ratio
•	Optimum total solids (TS) concentration: 7–9%.
•	Water helps in better mixing of biomass constituents, movement of bacteria, and faster digestion rate.
•	TOO HIGH water content: Reduces slurry temperature → gas production drops.
•	TOO LOW water content: Acids accumulate → prevents fermentation process (pH imbalance).

Parameter 4: pH Value
•	Optimum pH: 6.5–7.5 (slightly neutral).
•	Deviation from this range causes IMBALANCE in bacterial population, reducing gas production.
•	Acidogenic bacteria prefer lower pH; methanogens require neutral-slightly alkaline pH.

Parameter 5: Feeding Rate
•	If too much raw material is fed at once, acids will accumulate and the digestion process may STOP.
•	Faster feeding does NOT increase gas production.
•	For a retention period of 50 days: 1/50 of the digester volume should be fed daily.

Parameter 6: Carbon to Nitrogen (C/N) Ratio
•	Carbon (C) supplies energy for bacterial metabolism.
•	Nitrogen (N) is needed for building bacterial cell structures.
•	Anaerobic bacteria use carbon 25–30 times faster than nitrogen.
•	Optimum C:N ratio for maximum microbiological activity: 30:1.
•	C:N ratio < 20:1: Ammonia accumulation inhibits bacteria.
•	C:N ratio > 40:1: Carbon excess, slow digestion.
•	Different organic wastes have different C:N ratios — blending feedstocks optimizes performance.

Parameter 7: Seeding of Biomass with Bacteria
•	To accelerate the digestion process, a small amount of already-digested slurry (containing active methane-forming bacteria) is added to the fresh charge.
•	This is called SEEDING or INOCULATION.
•	Animal manure itself contains methane bacteria, but they multiply slowly. Seeding speeds up startup.

Parameter 8: Mixing/Stirring
•	Maintains UNIFORMITY in substrate concentration, temperature, and pH throughout the digester.
•	Minimizes formation of SCUM (floating crust) at the surface.
•	Prevents deposition of solids at the bottom of the digester.
•	Can be achieved by mechanical stirring, gas recirculation, or slurry recirculation.

Parameter 9: Retention Time
•	Retention time = Volume of digester ÷ Volume of slurry added per day.
•	Optimized to achieve 70–80% of complete digestion.
•	Typical retention time: 20–50 days (depends on temperature and biomass type).
•	The biodegradability period depends on temperature (higher temp = shorter retention time) and type of biomass (easily degradable vs. lignocellulosic).

Parameter 10: Effect of Toxic Substances
•	Toxic to biogas-producing bacteria: Ammonia (in high concentrations), antibiotics (in animal feed), pesticides, detergents.
•	Heavy metals (chromium, copper, nickel, zinc) in industrial wastes are toxic to bacteria at high concentrations.
•	Antibiotics present in animal feed trace into manure — avoid feedstocks from recently medicated animals.
